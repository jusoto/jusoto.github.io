What this means for UX

System 1 (fast) thinking handles routine interface interactions — tapping a familiar icon, dismissing a notification. System 2 (slow) kicks in when users hit something unfamiliar or must make a real decision. Good design minimizes unwanted System 2 load: clear labels, consistent patterns, and recognizable affordances keep users in the fast lane where they are happiest.

Tullis (1987) found that grouped layouts cut search time from 5.5s → 3.2s even at identical information density. The spacing itself encodes structure.

Design implications you observed

Spacing and grouping are free — they cost no pixels, no color, no animation — yet they dramatically reduce cognitive load. Every extra second a user spends searching is a second they are not completing their goal. Visual structure is doing the work that labels and instructions never could.

Activity 4 — Gulf of execution & evaluation builder

Scenario

A user wants to export their document as a PDF. For each stage of the interaction, choose whether the design bridges or widens the gulf.

Framework recap

The gulf of execution is the gap between what the user wants to do and the actions the interface makes available. The gulf of evaluation is the gap between what the system did and what the user can perceive. Bridging both requires: clear affordances + immediate, legible feedback.
